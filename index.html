    The Reality Behind the Simulation

    The real-world case was inspired by Global Financial Crisis 2008-2009

    What happened in reality?

    The Global Financial Crisis did not happen overnight. It developed gradually, from a period of easy credit and rising confidence into a full financial panic. The simulation you just played was designed to mirror that process across six phases.

    Phase 1 - Normal Market: Easy credit and rising confidence

    In the early 2000s, the U.S. housing market looked strong. Housing prices were rising, mortgage lending expanded, and major financial institutions were earning large profits from lending, securitization, and trading. Under low interest rates and easy credit conditions, more households were able to borrow, including subprime borrowers with weaker credit quality.

    Institutions such as Countrywide Financial expanded mortgage lending aggressively, while Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac played major roles in the housing finance system. At this stage, risk did not appear urgent. Defaults seemed manageable, credit markets were liquid, and many investors believed that mortgage risk had been reduced through diversification and financial engineering.

    Phase 2 - New Opportunity: Mortgages become investment products

    As investors searched for higher yield, mortgages were bundled into mortgage-backed securities, known as MBS, and more complex products such as collateralized debt obligations, or CDOs. These products were sold to banks, funds, insurance companies, and institutional investors around the world.

    Credit rating agencies such as Moody's, Standard & Poor's, and Fitch gave high ratings to many senior tranches, including AAA ratings. This made structured credit products appear safe and attractive. Major financial institutions such as Goldman Sachs, Merrill Lynch, Citigroup, Morgan Stanley, and Deutsche Bank were deeply involved in structuring, selling, or holding mortgage-related products.

    The market believed that risk had been transformed into safe, tradable assets through structuring, ratings, and diversification. In reality, the financial system was becoming more connected because many institutions were exposed to similar mortgage-related risks.

    Phase 3 - Boom & FOMO: Strong returns hide weak fundamentals

    During the boom, mortgage-related products delivered attractive returns. Investors who avoided them risked underperforming their peers, while funds and banks that held more structured credit appeared more profitable. This created pressure to keep participating in the trade.

    However, the quality of the underlying loans was deteriorating. Lending standards weakened, adjustable-rate mortgages became more common, and many borrowers depended on rising home prices to refinance their loans. By 2006, U.S. home prices had started to peak, but many market participants still believed that the problem was limited.

    At this stage, early warning signs existed, but they were easy to dismiss. Stress in subprime lending looked like an isolated problem rather than the beginning of a system-wide crisis.

    Phase 4 - Mixed Signals: Cracks appear and valuation becomes uncertain

    In 2007, the subprime mortgage problem became harder to ignore. New Century Financial, one of the largest subprime lenders in the United States, filed for bankruptcy. Two Bear Stearns hedge funds with heavy exposure to mortgage-backed securities also collapsed.

    The issue was no longer only about whether borrowers could repay. It became a valuation and confidence problem. Many mortgage-related products were difficult to price because there were fewer real buyers in the market. In August 2007, BNP Paribas froze withdrawals from three investment funds because it could not properly value some mortgage-related assets.

    The market entered a confusing stage. Some investors still believed the weakness was temporary or limited to lower-quality mortgages. Others became more cautious because liquidity was becoming unreliable, and model prices no longer clearly matched executable market prices.

    Phase 5 - Market Stress: Liquidity and funding pressure spread

    By 2008, liquidity stress had become severe. Institutions that relied on short-term funding were especially vulnerable because they held long-term and complex assets that could not be sold quickly without large discounts.

    Bear Stearns nearly collapsed in March 2008 and was sold to JPMorgan Chase with support from the Federal Reserve. Later that year, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac were placed into government conservatorship. AIG also came under extreme pressure because of its exposure to credit default swaps linked to mortgage securities.

    The crisis was no longer just about bad mortgages. It had become a liquidity, funding, and counterparty confidence crisis. Falling asset prices reduced confidence, weaker confidence reduced liquidity, and lower liquidity forced more selling. This feedback loop made the financial system increasingly fragile.

    Phase 6 - Crash & Reveal: Confidence breaks and the system freezes

    The crisis reached a systemic breaking point in September 2008 when Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy. Its collapse intensified panic across global financial markets because investors no longer knew which institutions were safe.

    Shortly after, AIG required a major rescue, and the U.S. government introduced emergency measures, including the Troubled Asset Relief Program, or TARP, which authorized up to $700 billion to stabilize the financial system. Banks became unwilling to lend to each other, credit markets froze, and the crisis spread into the real economy.

    The Great Recession lasted from December 2007 to June 2009. U.S. real GDP fell by about 4.3% from peak to trough, and unemployment rose sharply. The crisis revealed that a system that had looked profitable and well-managed was actually built on fragile assumptions about housing prices, liquidity, ratings, leverage, and trust.

    How this connects to your game

    In your game, the crisis unfolded in the same way: slowly at first, then suddenly. Early phases rewarded risk-taking. Later phases revealed weak borrower quality, crowded trades, liquidity pressure, margin calls, and systemic panic.

    The main challenge was not simply predicting the crash. The real challenge was deciding how much risk to take while the market still looked normal.

    Lessons learned

    • High returns can hide rising risk.
    • Liquidity matters most when markets are stressed.
    • Models and ratings cannot fully capture crisis conditions.
    • Crowded "safe" trades can become dangerous.
    • Short-term funding can make long-term assets fragile.
    • Good risk control means balancing return, evidence, liquidity, and survival.
    • The best crisis decisions are made before panic becomes obvious.

    Final Message

    Do not just ask: "How much can I earn?"

    Ask: "Can this portfolio survive when confidence disappears?"
